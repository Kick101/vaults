It is that time of the year for Sanjivavana to turn its colours to brown and turns its shape to flaky. For the past decade, it's been re-occurring phenomenon that every summer brings drought to the Sanjivavana. It is quite irony to the name, but it wasn't always like this. Sanjivavana was very apt to its name; rich green forest. But when Vinashdars arrived in the late 60s, they came along with the Karakhanas. Vinashdars are not typical practitioners you see around Sanjivavana. They have long flaky dry beards with incurable itchiness until they arrived here for a miracle that Sanjivavana was known for. Since their arrival the dynamic of Sanjivavana and its livelihood changed unprecedentedly. First arrivals of Vinashdars came with misery and hope. Once they tasted the miracle, they saw the becoming of Sanjivavana that no Vanastasthas ever imagined.

The ever bustling market is dry and dusted with brown soil. The very few farmers who are still left feeds the whole village but this time of the year, the crop yield is not of question of making a living but of survival. The yield is loss of investment but necessary for the survival. The little yield they farm barely fills the farmer families and their neighbors from whom they might have taken loan for the crop.
  
Every year an unseen disease takes away the infants along with their mothers. The wise men call these dark times that we must put faith in the deity. But man in pain becomes more outward than inward.

Madhavan is the only young farmer in the village, he picks up his tools and lunch and walks to this little farm about 2 acres that he inherited from his grandfather, the land is so precious to him that he stood against the Vinashdars' Karakhanas. He hadn't had much formal education but he's quick in picking up things. All he ever learned was from Sanjivavana. As he walks in the cracked dried lands which were once fertile, he recalls what his grandfather used to say "Land is blah blah".

After passing of his grandfather, he has only one companion, Bhairav, his black dog in he's late years.  Sooner or later even Bhairav would leave Madhavan. He has yet find someone to call his own, there's such someone that he admires afar, and writes poetry and letters, dusted in his attic. The only use he ever felt from learning from the tuition master that his grandfather insisted. He tries but fails to capture her eyes in his drawings with whom he's so dear in love.

'Rupali,' the young man thinks to himself.

Only if he had sold his land and took the job offered maybe he could have had a chance to ask her hand from her father but even then he's got no family that a father could send his daughter. 

Despite there's no farm work, he likes to sit under his trees in the farm and read books that his grandfather left him, for which Madhavan thought stupid to waste money in buying them but only now he understands the solitude in reading. Bhairav would occasionally chase after rats and cats that ponder in the farm.
